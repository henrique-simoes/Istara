    Thumb Zone Revisited: A Mixed-Methods Study of One-Handed Mobile
    Usability Across Device Sizes and Demographic Segments

    Maria Santos (1), David Chen (2), Fatima Al-Rashid (1), Kofi Mensah (3)

    (1) Department of Information Science, University of Sao Paulo, Brazil
    (2) Human-Computer Interaction Institute, Carnegie Mellon University, USA
    (3) School of Computing, University of Cape Town, South Africa

    Corresponding author: m.santos@usp.br
    Received: November 12, 2025 | Accepted: January 28, 2026 | Published: March 1, 2026

ABSTRACT
================================================================================

Background: The proliferation of large-screen smartphones (6.1-6.9 inches) has
challenged established models of one-handed mobile interaction, particularly
Steven Hoober's 2013 thumb zone model. As device sizes have increased by an
average of 22% since the original thumb zone research, understanding how users
adapt their grip, reach, and interaction strategies is critical for mobile
interface design.

Objective: This study investigates (a) how one-handed thumb reachability has
changed with modern device sizes, (b) whether demographic factors (age, hand
size, device familiarity) predict interaction success, and (c) what compensatory
strategies users employ when targets fall outside comfortable reach zones.

Methods: We conducted a mixed-methods study with 120 participants across three
countries (Brazil, USA, South Africa) using a custom instrumented testing
application. Participants completed 48 target acquisition tasks on their own
devices while we captured touch coordinates, grip pressure, hand repositioning
events, and task completion times. This was supplemented with 30 semi-structured
interviews exploring subjective comfort and strategy use.

Results: Our findings show that the comfortable one-handed thumb zone has shrunk
by approximately 31% relative to screen area compared to 2013 baselines.
Participants over 55 years old showed a 43% higher error rate for targets in
the upper-left quadrant (right-handed users). We identified four primary
compensatory strategies: device shimmy (67% of participants), index finger
assist (23%), chin stabilization (8%), and pinky shelf adjustment (54% used
concurrently with other strategies). Critical interaction targets placed in the
top 20% of the screen had a 2.3x higher error rate than those in the bottom 40%.

Conclusions: Current mobile UI conventions that place primary navigation and
action buttons in upper screen regions are misaligned with actual thumb
reachability on modern devices. We propose an updated thumb zone model with
device-size-specific recommendations and validate it against our empirical data
(R-squared = 0.84).

Keywords: mobile usability, thumb zone, one-handed interaction, touch targets,
          reachability, Fitts' law, smartphone ergonomics

================================================================================
1. INTRODUCTION
================================================================================

Mobile devices have become the primary computing platform for the majority of
the global population, with over 6.8 billion smartphone users as of 2025
(Statista, 2025). Despite this ubiquity, the ergonomic constraints of
one-handed smartphone interaction remain a persistent design challenge. The
seminal work by Hoober (2013) established the "thumb zone" model, which mapped
comfortable, stretchable, and hard-to-reach areas of the smartphone screen
based on observational research. This model has been widely adopted in mobile
design practice, influencing the placement of navigation bars, action buttons,
and interactive elements across millions of applications.

However, the mobile landscape has changed dramatically since 2013. The average
smartphone screen size has grown from 4.0 inches (diagonal) to 6.4 inches
(Counterpoint Research, 2025), representing a 60% increase in diagonal
measurement and approximately 156% increase in screen area. The introduction of
near-bezel-less designs, taller aspect ratios (from 16:9 to 19.5:9 and beyond),
and heavier devices (average weight increasing from 130g to 194g) has
fundamentally altered the physical interaction dynamics.

Several researchers have called for updated reachability models. Clark (2020)
proposed device-specific thumb zones but validated only on a single device.
Bergstrom-Lehtovirta and Oulasvirta (2014) developed a computational model of
thumb reach but did not account for demographic diversity or compensatory
strategies. More recently, Kim et al. (2024) examined thumb zone adaptations
for foldable devices but focused exclusively on young adult users in South Korea.

This paper addresses three research gaps. First, we provide empirical
reachability data across a diverse, multinational sample spanning a wide age
range (18-78 years). Second, we quantify the relationship between device size,
hand anthropometrics, and interaction performance. Third, we catalog and
categorize the compensatory strategies users employ when targets exceed their
comfortable reach, contributing to a more ecological understanding of mobile
interaction.

Our research questions are:
  RQ1: How does the effective thumb zone differ across modern device sizes
       (6.1", 6.4", 6.7", 6.9") compared to the 2013 baseline?
  RQ2: What demographic factors (age, hand span, device familiarity, country)
       predict one-handed interaction performance?
  RQ3: What compensatory strategies do users employ, and how do these strategies
       affect error rates and task completion times?

================================================================================
2. LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

2.1 Evolution of Touch Interaction Models

The study of touch interaction on mobile devices traces back to Fitts' (1954)
foundational work on aimed movement, which established that movement time is a
logarithmic function of the ratio between target distance and target width.
Parhi et al. (2006) were among the first to adapt Fitts' law to mobile touch
targets, recommending a minimum target size of 9.2mm for thumb interaction.
This recommendation was later reflected in platform guidelines, with Apple
recommending 44x44 point targets and Google recommending 48x48dp targets.

Hoober's (2013) observational study of 1,333 smartphone users in public spaces
found that 49% held their phones with one hand while using the thumb to interact,
36% used a cradle grip with the index finger, and 15% used two hands. The
resulting thumb zone model divided the screen into three regions: natural (green),
stretch (yellow), and hard-to-reach (red). This model was based primarily on
devices with 3.5-4.0 inch screens.

2.2 Critiques and Extensions

Several limitations of the original thumb zone model have been identified.
Nicolau and Jorge (2012) demonstrated that touch accuracy varies significantly
with age, with older adults showing larger touch offsets (mean 3.2mm vs. 1.8mm
for younger adults). Henze et al. (2011) analyzed over 100,000 touch events
from a deployed game and found systematic touch offset patterns that varied by
screen region, suggesting that different areas of the screen induce different
motor control strategies.

Wobbrock et al. (2008) introduced the concept of "ability-based design,"
arguing that interfaces should adapt to individual user capabilities rather
than assuming a normative interaction model. This perspective is particularly
relevant to thumb zone research, as hand size, grip strength, and dexterity
vary substantially across the population.

2.3 Compensatory Strategies

Limited research has examined how users adapt when targets are difficult to
reach. Eardley and Roudaut (2016) identified "device microadjustments" as a
common strategy, where users subtly shift the device position within their
palm to extend thumb reach. Le et al. (2018) documented "clutching" behavior,
analogous to mouse clutching, where users lift and reposition their thumb to
reach distant targets. However, a comprehensive taxonomy of compensatory
strategies across diverse populations has not been established.

2.4 Cross-Cultural Considerations

Mobile interaction research has been predominantly conducted with participants
from North America, Europe, and East Asia. Bidwell et al. (2014) highlighted
the importance of including participants from sub-Saharan Africa, where
different device ownership patterns (more shared devices, more budget
smartphones) may influence interaction behaviors. Similarly, mobile usage
patterns in Brazil differ substantially from North American norms, with
longer average daily screen time (5.4 hours vs. 4.1 hours) and higher
proportions of mobile-only internet users (Cetic.br, 2025).

================================================================================
3. METHODOLOGY
================================================================================

3.1 Participants

We recruited 120 participants (40 per country site) using a stratified sampling
approach to ensure diversity across age, gender, hand dominance, and device size.

Demographics:
- Age: M=38.7, SD=15.2, Range=18-78
- Gender: 58 female, 54 male, 8 non-binary/other
- Hand dominance: 104 right-handed (87%), 14 left-handed (12%), 2 ambidextrous
- Hand span (thumb tip to pinky tip): M=20.3cm, SD=2.8cm, Range=15.1-26.4cm
- Device sizes: 6.1" (n=34), 6.4" (n=38), 6.7" (n=31), 6.9" (n=17)

Inclusion criteria: owned a smartphone for at least 1 year, used the device
daily, no motor impairments affecting hand use.

3.2 Apparatus

We developed "ThumbTrack," a custom Android/iOS application that presents
circular targets at predetermined screen coordinates and captures:
- Touch coordinates (x, y) at 120Hz
- Touch pressure (where supported by hardware)
- Accelerometer data (to detect device repositioning)
- Gyroscope data (to detect device tilting)
- Grip change events (detected via edge touch sensor patterns)
- Task completion time (ms)
- Error events (misses, slips, accidental activations)

The application was installed on participants' own devices to ensure ecological
validity of grip patterns and device familiarity.

3.3 Procedure

Each session lasted approximately 60 minutes and followed this protocol:

Phase 1 — Anthropometric Measurement (5 min):
Hand span, thumb length, and grip strength measured using digital calipers
and a dynamometer.

Phase 2 — Calibration (5 min):
Participants performed 12 practice trials to establish baseline grip and
normalize accelerometer data.

Phase 3 — Target Acquisition Tasks (30 min):
48 targets presented in a balanced latin-square design across:
- 8 screen regions (4 quadrants x 2 vertical zones: upper/lower)
- 3 target sizes (7mm, 10mm, 14mm diameter)
- 2 repetitions per condition
Targets appeared at screen-size-normalized positions.
Inter-trial interval: 1.5 seconds.
Instruction: "Tap the target as quickly and accurately as possible using
only your thumb, holding the phone in one hand."

Phase 4 — Semi-Structured Interview (20 min, n=30 subset):
Conducted with every fourth participant. Topics: comfort perceptions,
strategy awareness, design preferences, frustrating interactions.

3.4 Data Analysis

Quantitative data were analyzed using mixed-effects regression models with
participant as a random effect. Fixed effects included device size, screen
region, target size, age group, hand span, and country. We used Bonferroni
correction for multiple comparisons. Effect sizes are reported as Cohen's d
or partial eta-squared as appropriate.

Qualitative interview data were analyzed using reflexive thematic analysis
(Braun & Clarke, 2019) with two independent coders. Initial inter-coder
agreement was 82%; disagreements were resolved through discussion.

3.5 Ethical Approval

The study was approved by the ethics committees at all three institutions
(USP Protocol #2025-0847, CMU IRB #2025-1234, UCT HREC #789/2025).
All participants provided written informed consent.

================================================================================

A mixed-effects logistic regression confirmed significant main effects of
screen region (chi-sq(7) = 487.3, p < .001), device size (chi-sq(3) = 89.2,
p < .001), and their interaction (chi-sq(21) = 43.7, p = .003).

Compared to Hoober's 2013 baseline (estimated from published figures), the
comfortable zone (less than 5% error rate) has shrunk from approximately 58% of
screen area to 27% on 6.7" devices — a 31 percentage point reduction.

4.2 Demographic Predictors (RQ2)

Age was a significant predictor of error rate (beta = 0.018, SE = 0.003,
p < .001), with each additional year of age associated with a 1.8% increase
in odds of error. Participants over 55 (n=28) had 43% higher error rates in
the upper-left quadrant compared to those under 35 (n=42).

Hand span was a significant predictor (beta = -0.12, SE = 0.02, p < .001),
with each centimeter increase in hand span associated with a 12% decrease
in error odds. The interaction between hand span and device size was also
significant (p = .008), indicating that hand span mattered more for larger
devices.

Country was not a significant predictor after controlling for age and hand
span (p = .34), suggesting that the physical ergonomics of thumb interaction
are consistent across cultural contexts.

Device familiarity (measured as months of ownership) was marginally significant
(p = .051), with a trend toward fewer errors with longer ownership.

4.3 Compensatory Strategies (RQ3)

Through video analysis and accelerometer/gyroscope data, we identified four
primary compensatory strategies:

Strategy 1: Device Shimmy (67% of participants)
Users slide the device upward in their palm to extend thumb reach toward the
top of the screen. This was detected as a rapid downward accelerometer spike
followed by stabilization. Mean repositioning time: 340ms (SD=120ms). This
strategy reduced upper-region error rates by 38% but increased overall task
time by 22%.

Strategy 2: Pinky Shelf (54% of participants)
Users rest the bottom of the device on their extended pinky finger, creating
a stable base that allows the palm to slide down. This was used concurrently
with other strategies and was more common among users with larger devices
(72% of 6.7"+ users vs. 41% of 6.1" users).

Strategy 3: Index Finger Assist (23% of participants)
Users temporarily switch from thumb-only to index-finger interaction for
hard-to-reach targets. This requires a complete grip change. Mean grip change
time: 890ms (SD=310ms). While this eliminates reachability errors, the grip
transition time significantly increases overall task time.

Strategy 4: Chin Stabilization (8% of participants)
Users press the top of the device against their chin to stabilize it while
reaching with the thumb. This was observed almost exclusively in participants
over 50 and only with devices 6.7" or larger.

Interview data revealed that 73% of participants were unaware of their own
compensatory strategies until prompted to reflect. Comments included
descriptions of unconscious adaptation that had become habitual over time.

4.4 Target Size Effects

Following Fitts' law predictions, smaller targets yielded higher error rates
and longer completion times. The effect was amplified in hard-to-reach regions:

Table 3: Error Rate by Target Size and Reachability Zone
+-------------+---------+---------+---------+
| Target Size | Easy    | Stretch | Hard    |
+-------------+---------+---------+---------+
| 7mm         |  4.8%   | 14.2%   | 32.7%   |
| 10mm        |  2.1%   |  8.7%   | 21.4%   |
| 14mm        |  1.2%   |  4.3%   | 13.8%   |
+-------------+---------+---------+---------+

The interaction between target size and zone was significant (F(4, 5604) = 23.8,
p < .001, partial eta-sq = .017), indicating that larger targets are especially
beneficial in hard-to-reach zones.

================================================================================
5. DISCUSSION
================================================================================

5.1 Implications for Mobile Design

Our findings have direct implications for mobile interface design. The most
critical recommendation is to place primary interactive elements in the bottom
40% of the screen, where error rates remain below 5% across all tested device
sizes. This contradicts the common iOS pattern of placing the navigation bar
and primary actions in the top region of the screen.

The significant effect of target size in hard-to-reach zones suggests that when
top-screen placement is unavoidable, designers should use larger touch targets
(minimum 14mm) and generous padding. The "stretch" zone can remain usable if
targets are appropriately sized.

Our compensatory strategy findings suggest that users are remarkably adaptive,
but this adaptation comes at a cost: increased task time, reduced efficiency,
and potentially increased physical strain. Design should minimize the need for
compensatory behaviors rather than relying on user adaptation.

5.2 The Updated Thumb Zone Model

Based on our empirical data, we propose an updated thumb zone model that is
parameterized by device size. For a 6.4" device (the current mode):

- Comfortable zone (less than 5% error): Bottom 40% of screen, center-biased
- Stretch zone (5-15% error): Middle 30% of screen + bottom corners
- Hard-to-reach zone (over 15% error): Top 30% of screen

These zones shift proportionally with device size, expanding the hard-to-reach
zone by approximately 5 percentage points per 0.3" increase in screen diagonal.

5.3 Limitations

Several limitations should be noted. First, our target acquisition task is a
simplified abstraction of real app interaction, which involves scrolling,
swiping, and complex gestures in addition to tapping. Second, our lab setting
may not capture the full range of real-world contexts (walking, lying down,
carrying items). Third, our sample, while multinational, was predominantly
urban and may not generalize to all populations. Fourth, foldable devices
and tablets were excluded from this study.

5.4 Future Work

Future research should examine thumb zone dynamics during continuous interaction
(scrolling, swiping) rather than discrete target acquisition. Longitudinal
studies could track how users adapt to new devices over weeks and months.
Adaptive interfaces that dynamically adjust UI element placement based on
detected grip patterns represent a promising design direction that could
leverage our compensatory strategy taxonomy.

================================================================================
6. CONCLUSIONS
================================================================================

This study provides updated empirical data on one-handed mobile interaction
across 120 participants, three countries, and four device size categories.
Our key contributions are:

1. An updated thumb zone model showing a 31% shrinkage of the comfortable
   interaction area on modern 6.7" devices compared to 2013 baselines.

2. Quantified demographic effects, with age (1.8% increased error odds per
   year) and hand span (12% decreased error odds per cm) as significant
   predictors of interaction success.

3. A taxonomy of four compensatory strategies (device shimmy, pinky shelf,
   index finger assist, chin stabilization) with associated performance
   characteristics.

4. Evidence that target sizes of at least 14mm in hard-to-reach zones can
   reduce error rates by 58% compared to the platform-minimum 7mm targets.

These findings underscore the need for the mobile design community to move
beyond legacy placement conventions and embrace bottom-oriented, thumb-friendly
interface architectures that account for the ergonomic realities of modern
large-screen devices.
